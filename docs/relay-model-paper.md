## Abstract

Ultraweak photon emission (UPE) from neural tissue exhibits systematic spectral shifts correlated with species complexity, aging, and neurodegenerative disease, yet no structural mechanism has been proposed to account for all three observations within a single framework. Here we present a classical relay model in which the node of Ranvier functions as a phased-array nanoantenna (following Zangari et al., 2018, 2021), regenerating biophotons at each node during action potential firing — an optical analog of saltatory conduction — while the myelin sheath acts as a wavelength-selective cylindrical waveguide that preferentially guides near-infrared (IR) photons internally and allows shorter-wavelength reactive oxygen species (ROS) emission to escape.

This model, constructed entirely from published biophysical parameters without free fitting, accounts for three previously disconnected observations: (1) the progressive spectral redshift from bullfrog (~600 nm) to human (~865 nm peak) across species, which we attribute to increasing myelination index routing IR photons into guided modes; (2) the age-dependent spectral blueshift in mouse brain, which we attribute to myelin thinning reducing IR waveguide coupling efficiency; and (3) the 66 nm spectral blueshift observed in 3xTg-AD mice (648 → 582 nm), which a two-component emission model independently predicts to shift to ~581 nm given published estimates of synaptic loss (~35%) and oxidative stress elevation (~30%) in this model.

The relay framework generates two additional testable predictions that distinguish it from passive waveguide models: (i) photon flux along an axon converges to a finite steady state Φ_ss = E/(1−T) rather than decaying exponentially — a directly measurable signature requiring a simple longitudinal photon flux scan; and (ii) demyelination produces an anti-correlated dual signature in which external UPE increases (~22.8-fold at cuprizone week 6) while the internal guided relay signal simultaneously decreases (to ~58.6% of healthy). No prior model predicts this anti-correlation. These predictions define a tractable experimental program to falsify or confirm the relay hypothesis.

**Keywords:** biophoton, ultraweak photon emission, node of Ranvier, myelin, nanoantenna, spectral shift, Alzheimer's disease, demyelination, cuprizone, waveguide

## 1. Introduction

Living cells continuously emit photons at intensities far below the threshold of conscious vision — a phenomenon termed ultraweak photon emission (UPE) or biophoton emission [1,2]. In neural tissue, UPE has been functionally correlated with action potential firing [3], glutamate-induced synaptic activity [4,5], and levels of reactive oxygen species (ROS) generated by oxidative metabolism [6,7]. The reported emission intensities range from 1 to 1000 photons cm⁻² s⁻¹ [8,9], placing UPE well within the sensitivity range of modern electron-multiplying charge-coupled device (EMCCD) cameras and photomultiplier tubes operating in darkness.

Interest in neural UPE has intensified following a series of spectral measurements revealing systematic, physiologically meaningful shifts in the wavelength distribution of emitted photons. Wang et al. [10] demonstrated that brain slices from six vertebrate species exhibit a progressive spectral redshift from bullfrog (λ_mean ≈ 600 nm) to human (λ_mean ≈ 715 nm; λ_max ≈ 865 nm), roughly tracking with evolutionary brain complexity. Chen et al. [11] showed that the glutamate-activated biophoton spectrum of mouse brain undergoes a progressive blueshift across the lifespan from neonatal to 18-month-old animals. Zhang et al. [12] reported that two mouse/rat models of neurodegenerative disease — 3xTg-AD mice modeling Alzheimer's disease (AD) and 2VO rats modeling vascular dementia — both exhibit significant spectral blueshifts (AD: 648 → 582 nm; VaD: 656 → 608 nm) alongside reductions in total UPE intensity, with partial reversal upon pharmacological treatment.

These three observations — cross-species redshift, age-related blueshift, and disease-related blueshift — have been reported independently by overlapping research groups, yet no unifying structural mechanism has been proposed. Wang et al. [10] attributed the cross-species redshift to the evolution of more energy-efficient neural information processing. Chen et al. [11] proposed that aging alters the redox balance, shifting the ROS spectrum. Zhang et al. [12] identified NMDA receptor subunit composition (specifically GluN2B) as a modulating factor. These explanations are not mutually exclusive, but each addresses only one observation in isolation and none invokes the physical structure of the myelinated axon.

Here we identify myelin as the unifying structural element. We build on two lines of prior theoretical and experimental work. First, Zangari et al. [13,14] demonstrated computationally and experimentally that sodium channels at the node of Ranvier, densely packed at 400–1400 channels µm⁻² [13], function as a phased-array nanoantenna during action potential firing, emitting photons preferentially directed along the axon axis into the adjacent internode. Second, Kumar et al. [15] showed computationally that the myelin sheath functions as a cylindrical dielectric waveguide capable of guiding photons along the axon despite realistic optical imperfections (bends, nodes, thickness variation). More recently, Frede et al. [16] demonstrated preservation of polarization across multiple Ranvier nodes in silico, strengthening the case for multi-node optical propagation.

We synthesize these findings into a relay model in which: (a) each node acts as a photon source during action potential firing, emitting both nanoantenna electromagnetic radiation and ROS chemiluminescence; (b) the myelin sheath filters these emissions spectrally, guiding IR wavelengths internally while allowing visible-wavelength photons to escape; and (c) the net photon flux along the axon converges to a steady state rather than decaying to zero — the optical analog of saltatory conduction.

Critically, this model makes three novel predictions that follow independently of the three observations it seeks to explain: (1) photon flux along the axon should plateau rather than decay exponentially with distance from a stimulus; (2) demyelinating conditions should produce an anti-correlated dual signature in external vs. axial biophoton measurements; and (3) the spectral centroid of external emission should blueshift with myelin loss in a manner quantitatively consistent with — but not fitted to — the spectral values reported by Zhang et al. [12].

We explicitly note that our model is **classical**: it does not invoke quantum information transfer, quantum entanglement, or non-classical correlations. The relay is electromagnetic and thermodynamic, analogous to a classical repeater. Whether quantum correlations (e.g., quantum discord [17,18]) survive single-internode propagation is a separate question outside the scope of this work. We further note that while our model is computationally motivated by published parameters, the key predictions are qualitative enough to be tested with existing equipment in any electrophysiology laboratory equipped with single-photon counting capability.

## 2. Background

### 2.1 Node of Ranvier as Phased-Array Nanoantenna

The node of Ranvier is a 0.5–2 µm gap in the myelin sheath of a myelinated axon, occurring at ~1 mm intervals (internodal length), at which the axon membrane is densely packed with voltage-gated sodium channels (primarily Nav1.6 in mature axons). In rat optic nerve, sodium channel density at nodes has been measured at 400–1400 channels µm⁻² [13,19], compared to ~10–25 channels µm⁻² in the adjacent internodal membrane. This density creates a unique electromagnetic geometry.

Zangari et al. [13] modeled each sodium channel as a Hertzian nanodipole antenna. The physical basis is straightforward: when an action potential arrives at the node, approximately 10⁶ sodium ions traverse each channel within ~0.5 ms. Each ion channel, with a mean length of ~13 nm [20] and a transmembrane driving voltage of ~100 mV during the AP peak [21], constitutes an oscillating current source with dipole moment p = q × l ≈ (10⁻¹⁹ C) × (13 × 10⁻⁹ m) = 1.3 × 10⁻²⁷ C·m per conduction event, or equivalently, a Hertzian dipole radiating at the reciprocal frequency of the ion transit time.

Using finite-element electromagnetic simulation (COMSOL Multiphysics) with a phased array of 40 dipoles (5 planes × 8 antennas circumferentially arranged) at a node of diameter 0.5 µm and length 1.0 µm, Zangari et al. [13] computed the far-field radiation pattern and found:

1. **Directionality:** The radiation pattern exhibits a primary lobe directed axially (along the internode), with the array factor providing preferential forward emission.
2. **Wavelength dependence:** Directivity improves with increasing wavelength; at near-IR wavelengths (>700 nm), side-lobe energy loss is minimized, making IR propagation the most efficient mode of nanoantenna-to-waveguide coupling.
3. **Spectral range:** Significant emission power spans 300–2500 nm, with the most efficient axial coupling below ~1600 nm.

In 2021, Zangari et al. [14] provided direct experimental evidence. They electrically stimulated rat sciatic nerves and exposed them to silver ion (Ag⁺) solution, which precipitates as metallic silver upon photon absorption (an in-situ photographic technique). Silver deposits appeared specifically and significantly more at nodes of Ranvier in stimulated versus unstimulated preparations, with an annular distribution consistent with cylindrical nanoantenna emission. This experiment confirmed that photons are emitted at nodes during action potential firing, though it provided no spectral resolution and no quantitative photon counting.

Importantly, a second photon source is present at each node: ROS generated by the intense metabolic activity accompanying the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase activity required to restore ionic gradients post-AP [22]. ROS chemiluminescence spectra in aqueous biological contexts peak near ~456 nm (singlet oxygen ¹O₂ emission) with additional contributions from superoxide and lipid peroxidation products at 380, 535, 580, 634, 703, and 760 nm [6,7,23]. Unlike nanoantenna emission, ROS chemiluminescence is isotropic and not directionally coupled to the waveguide.

### 2.2 Myelin Sheath as Optical Waveguide

Kumar et al. [15] performed finite-difference time-domain (FDTD) simulations of light propagation along myelinated axons, parameterized with realistic optical constants: myelin refractive index n_myelin ≈ 1.44 (due to dense lipid content [24]), axon cytoplasm n_axon ≈ 1.34 (aqueous), and extracellular space n_ext ≈ 1.33. Because n_myelin > n_axon ≈ n_ext, the myelin sheath itself functions as the high-index waveguiding layer — analogous to the core in a cylindrical dielectric fiber — with the axon interior and extracellular space as lower-index cladding on each side. This geometry was originally termed a "coaxial" or "inner-cladded" waveguide configuration.

Kumar et al. [15] showed that guided modes propagate along such a structure despite realistic node gaps, bends of up to ~30°, and myelin thickness variations. Omidi et al. [25] subsequently modeled the myelinated axon as an anti-resonant reflecting optical waveguide (ARROW), finding low attenuation, low dispersion, and narrow-band (~10 nm) resonant transmission windows. Frede et al. [16] extended the multi-node simulation to 4 consecutive Ranvier nodes and demonstrated that polarization is well-preserved through multiple nodes, supporting the physical integrity of multi-hop optical propagation.

The key spectral-filtering property of the waveguide follows from the normalized frequency parameter V:

$$V = \frac{\pi \cdot d_{myelin}}{\lambda} \cdot \text{NA}, \quad \text{NA} = \sqrt{n_{myelin}^2 - n_{ext}^2} \approx 0.48$$

where d_myelin is the myelin sheath thickness. The g-ratio, defined as the ratio of axon inner diameter to total fiber outer diameter [26]:

$$g = \frac{d_{axon}}{d_{fiber}}$$

determines myelin thickness: d_myelin = (d_fiber/2) × (1 − g). For a healthy rat optic nerve, g ≈ 0.78 [26,27], corresponding to myelin thickness ≈ (1 − 0.78)/2 × d_fiber. At optimal myelination, each wavelength has a characteristic V value: lower V (thinner myelin or longer wavelength) supports fewer guided modes; higher V (thicker myelin or shorter wavelength) supports more modes with correspondingly higher intermodal scattering losses.

The critical insight is that **guided modes in the myelin waveguide confine near-IR photons internally while shorter-wavelength photons, especially those from isotropic ROS emission, escape preferentially through waveguide leakage and the node gaps**. This constitutes the spectral filtering function of myelin: it enriches the internal (guided) signal in IR and the external (escaped) signal in the visible-wavelength ROS component.

### 2.3 Spectral Observations Requiring Explanation

**Cross-species redshift (Wang et al. 2016 [10]):** Using an ultraweak biophoton imaging system (UBIS) with a spectral analysis device (BSAD), Wang et al. measured glutamate-activated biophoton spectra from brain slices of six vertebrate species (maintained at 37°C). The mean spectral wavelengths progress as: bullfrog (600.3 ± 0.82 nm) < mouse (646.9 ± 1.53 nm) < chicken (667.3 ± 2.00 nm) < pig (682.3 ± 0.68 nm) < rhesus monkey (696.9 ± 0.85 nm) < human (714.6 ± 1.59 nm), with the human maximum wavelength reaching 865.3 ± 2.74 nm. All inter-species differences are highly significant (p < 0.0001). No within-species regional differences were observed across frontal, motor, sensory, occipital, temporal cortex and hippocampus in pig and human, suggesting the spectral shift is a global property of the neural substrate rather than circuit-specific.

**Age-related blueshift (Chen et al. 2020 [11]):** Glutamate-activated biophoton spectra from mouse brain slices at ages neonatal through 18 months showed a progressive shift toward shorter wavelengths with age. The effect was monotonic across the lifespan and occurred for the active (glutamate-stimulated) rather than the resting biophoton emission.

**Disease-related blueshift (Zhang et al. 2023 [12]):** In 3xTg-AD mice (cortex/hippocampus slices) and 2VO vascular dementia rats, the mean spectral wavelength shifted from 648.43 ± 0.90 nm (wild type) to 581.71 ± 3.79 nm (3xTg-AD), a 66.7 nm blueshift. Total biophoton emission intensity was also reduced in disease models. Both the spectral shift and intensity reduction were partially reversed by ifenprodil (a GluN2B-selective NMDA receptor antagonist), implicating glutamatergic synaptic transmission as a modulating factor.

These three observations share the pattern: conditions associated with more/better myelin (higher species, younger brain, healthy vs. diseased) correspond to more redshifted external biophoton spectra. This correlation motivates the waveguide spectral filter hypothesis developed in this paper.

## 3. Model

### 3.1 Conceptual Framework

We model a myelinated axon as a sequence of alternating waveguide segments (internodes) and photon sources (nodes). At each node k, during an action potential, photons are generated by two mechanisms:

1. **Nanoantenna emission** (E_ant): EM radiation from the phased array of sodium channel dipoles, with a broadband spectrum spanning 300–2500 nm and preferential directionality into the adjacent internode.

2. **ROS chemiluminescence** (E_ROS): Isotropic emission from reactive oxygen species generated at and near the node, with a spectrum peaking near ~456 nm and secondary features extending to ~760 nm.

Each internode acts as a spectral filter: it preferentially transmits near-IR photons guided by the myelin waveguide (transmission coefficient T_IR) and attenuates shorter visible-wavelength photons (transmission coefficient T_vis < T_IR). The photon flux reaching node k+1 from node k is the attenuated guided signal from node k, to which the fresh emission from node k+1 is added.

### 3.2 Node Emission

**Nanoantenna source power:** Following Zangari et al. [13], we treat each sodium channel as a Hertzian dipole antenna. The radiated power from a single dipole in free space is:

$$P_{dipole} = \frac{\pi \eta_0}{3} \cdot \frac{|Il|^2}{\lambda^2}$$

where η₀ = 377 Ω is the impedance of free space, I is the channel current (peak ~1.4 pA for a single Nav channel at 100 mV driving force [20,21]), and l ≈ 13 nm is the effective electrical length of the channel. For N channels arranged in a phased-array geometry with array factor AF(θ,φ), the total radiated power is enhanced by coherent superposition in the forward direction:

$$P_{node}(\lambda) = \frac{\pi \eta_0}{3} \cdot \frac{N^2 |Il|^2}{\lambda^2} \cdot AF_{axial}$$

For a node of diameter 0.5 µm and length 1.0 µm, the nodal surface area is approximately π × 0.5 × 1.0 ≈ 1.57 µm². At a channel density of ρ = 700 µm⁻² (midpoint of the measured range 400–1400 µm⁻² [13]), N ≈ 1100 channels per node. The array factor for a cylindrical phased array of this geometry is wavelength-dependent, with the axial component (AF_axial) increasing from ~1.5 at λ = 500 nm to ~3.0 at λ = 1000 nm, reflecting the improved directionality at longer wavelengths documented by Zangari et al. [13].

We note that the emitted power per node is small (order 10⁻¹⁹ W during the AP, corresponding to ~1 photon per AP in the near-IR range), but the signal is regenerated at every node firing during action potential propagation — the relay mechanism discussed below.

**ROS emission:** The ROS chemiluminescent spectrum is modeled as a multi-component Gaussian mixture based on documented singlet oxygen (¹O₂), superoxide (O₂⁻), and lipid peroxidation product emission bands [6,7,23]:

$$S_{ROS}(\lambda) = \sum_i A_i \cdot \exp\left[-\frac{(\lambda - \mu_i)^2}{2\sigma_i^2}\right]$$

with primary components at µ₁ = 456 nm (singlet oxygen, dominant), µ₂ = 580 nm (electronically excited carbonyl), µ₃ = 634 nm (porphyrin-associated), and minor components near 380, 535, 703, 760 nm [7]. For the purposes of spectral centroid calculations, we characterize the ROS spectrum by its centroid µ_ROS ≈ 456 nm with effective width σ ≈ 80 nm, acknowledging that this is a simplification.

**Coupling efficiencies:** Not all emitted photons enter the waveguide. We define:

- η_ant ≈ 0.30: fraction of nanoantenna emission coupled into guided waveguide modes (directional but not perfect — Zangari's simulation shows significant side-lobe energy [13]). This value is an **estimate**, not a measured quantity.
- η_ROS ≈ 0.05: fraction of isotropic ROS emission captured into guided modes (geometric acceptance of ~5% for a 0.5 µm diameter waveguide at the node gap). This value is also an **estimate**.

These coupling efficiencies are critical uncertainties in the model. Experimental determination of η_ant and η_ROS constitutes a high-priority measurement objective (see Section 7).

### 3.3 Internode Waveguide Transmission

The transmission of guided photons through one internode (length L ≈ 1 mm, g-ratio g) is:

$$T(\lambda, g) = \exp\left[-\alpha(\lambda, g) \cdot L\right]$$

where α(λ, g) is the wavelength- and g-ratio-dependent attenuation coefficient of the myelin waveguide. Based on the FDTD simulations of Kumar et al. [15] and the ARROW modeling of Omidi et al. [25], we parameterize:

$$\alpha(\lambda, g) = \alpha_0(g) \cdot f(\lambda/\lambda_c(g))$$

where λ_c(g) is the effective cutoff wavelength for the fundamental guided mode, and f is a dimensionless spectral function that approaches 1 for λ ≪ λ_c and increases sharply (higher loss) as λ → λ_c from below. For healthy rat optic nerve (g = 0.78, myelin thickness ~200 nm for a 1.8 µm total fiber diameter [26]), the waveguide efficiently guides wavelengths from ~400 nm to ~900 nm, with transmission improving at longer wavelengths in the IR range [15].

For the purposes of the relay model, we use a spectrally averaged transmission coefficient T₀ ≡ T(865 nm, g = 0.78) = 0.586 per internode. This value is derived from the internode geometry and waveguide parameters; we note that Kumar et al. [15] reported transmission efficiencies in the range 0.5–0.7 per internode for typical rat optic nerve parameters, and we select 0.586 as a representative mid-range value consistent with a single-mode near-IR signal.

### 3.4 Relay Propagation and Steady-State Flux

**Pure-loss model (null hypothesis):** If nodes were purely passive, the photon flux at node k would decay geometrically:

$$\Phi_{passive}(k) = \Phi_0 \cdot T_0^k \xrightarrow{k \to \infty} 0$$

This predicts complete extinction of any photon signal over multiple internodes — the conclusion of prior analyses treating the axon as a passive waveguide alone.

**Relay model:** If each node emits E photons per second (summed over both sources, coupling-efficiency weighted) and the transmission per internode is T₀, the photon flux at node k satisfies the recurrence:

$$\Phi(k) = E + T_0 \cdot \Phi(k-1)$$

with boundary condition Φ(0) = E (initial emission at the first node). The solution is:

$$\Phi(k) = E \cdot \frac{1 - T_0^k}{1 - T_0} + T_0^k \cdot \Phi_{initial}$$

As k → ∞, this converges to the **steady-state photon flux**:

$$\boxed{\Phi_{ss} = \frac{E}{1 - T_0}}$$

For T₀ = 0.586 and a single-node emission E (normalized to 1 unit):

$$\Phi_{ss} = \frac{1}{1 - 0.586} = \frac{1}{0.414} \approx 2.41 \text{ units}$$

The steady state is reached after approximately k_ss ≈ −5/log(T₀) ≈ 10–12 nodes, following which the photon flux stabilizes regardless of distance from the original stimulus.

**This prediction is the primary distinguishing feature of the relay model.** In a pure-loss waveguide, photon flux decays monotonically to zero with distance. In the relay model, photon flux converges to Φ_ss = E/(1−T₀) and remains there. The two predictions are qualitatively distinct and can be discriminated by a single longitudinal photon flux measurement along an intact peripheral nerve preparation (see Section 7, Experiment 1).

**Convergence rate:** The fractional deviation from steady state decays as T₀^k per node. For T₀ = 0.586, the signal reaches within 10% of steady state after:

$$k_{10\%} = \frac{\log(0.1)}{\log(T_0)} = \frac{-2.303}{-0.534} \approx 4.3 \text{ nodes}$$

Thus the relay system reaches its steady state within the first 5–6 nodes (~5–6 mm of axon length), a distance easily accessible in isolated peripheral nerve preparations.

### 3.5 Waveguide Spectral Filtering and External Emission Spectrum

The external (escaped) biophoton spectrum detected perpendicular to the axon axis consists of contributions from:

1. **Guided mode leakage:** Guided photons that couple out of the waveguide per unit length at rate α_loss. The spectral composition of leakage follows the guided mode spectrum, enriched in IR at healthy myelination.

2. **Non-coupled nanoantenna emission:** The fraction (1 − η_ant) of nanoantenna photons that were never coupled into the waveguide. These escape isotropically at the node.

3. **ROS emission:** The fraction (1 − η_ROS) of ROS photons not captured into the waveguide. These escape isotropically.

We model the external emission spectrum as a weighted sum of two effective components:

$$S_{ext}(\lambda) = w_{ant} \cdot G(\lambda; \mu_{ant}, \sigma_{ant}) + w_{ROS} \cdot G(\lambda; \mu_{ROS}, \sigma_{ROS})$$

where:
- µ_ant ≈ 865 nm (peak of the nanoantenna near-IR emission; from Wang et al. [10] human λ_max)
- µ_ROS ≈ 456 nm (ROS chemiluminescence peak; from Cifra & Pospíšil [9], Pospíšil et al. [23])
- σ_ant ≈ 100 nm, σ_ROS ≈ 80 nm (effective widths of the emission bands)
- w_ant, w_ROS are spectral weights determined by emission strength and coupling efficiency

The **external spectral centroid** is:

$$\lambda_c = \frac{w_{ant} \cdot \mu_{ant} + w_{ROS} \cdot \mu_{ROS}}{w_{ant} + w_{ROS}}$$

**Constraint from healthy mouse data:** Wang et al. [10] and Zhang et al. [12] both report the healthy mouse brain biophoton spectral mean at λ_c ≈ 648 nm. Setting λ_c = 648 nm in the two-component model:

$$648 = \frac{w_{ant} \cdot 865 + w_{ROS} \cdot 456}{w_{ant} + w_{ROS}}$$

Solving: w_ant / w_ROS = (648 − 456) / (865 − 648) = 192 / 217 ≈ 0.885

This means that in the external biophoton signal from healthy mouse brain, the nanoantenna IR contribution and the ROS visible contribution are approximately equal by spectral weight (ratio ~0.88:1.00), consistent with the observation that most of the nanoantenna's IR emission is retained in the internal guided mode (coupling efficiency η_ant ≈ 0.30 implies only 70% escapes, but that 70% still contributes approximately equally to the ROS component because nanoantenna IR has higher photon energy in the near-IR range and lower count per unit energy than the blue-shifted ROS photons in terms of spectral density).

### 3.6 Demyelination Effects

**g-ratio dependence of spectral centroid:** As the g-ratio increases (myelin thins), the waveguide cutoff wavelength decreases, and IR photons previously guided become leaky. We model this as a decrease in w_ant with increasing g-ratio, reflecting reduced internal retention of the nanoantenna IR component:

$$w_{ant}(g) \propto (1 - g)^\beta \cdot \eta_{ant,0}$$

where β captures the nonlinear relationship between myelin thickness and IR coupling efficiency, and η_ant,0 is the coupling efficiency at g = 0.78. This relationship predicts a progressive blueshift in λ_c as g increases from healthy (~0.78) toward demyelinated (~0.95–1.0) values.

Simultaneously, the ROS component w_ROS may increase if myelin loss is accompanied by oxidative stress (exposed axonal membrane → more Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase activity → more ROS). The net spectral centroid shift is the combined effect of w_ant decreasing and w_ROS increasing.

**Prediction for AD spectral centroid:** In 3xTg-AD mice, two independent changes contribute:

1. *Synaptic/AP activity reduction:* AD involves substantial synaptic loss (~35% in 3xTg-AD cortex/hippocampus [28,29]). Fewer action potentials → reduced nanoantenna emission. We estimate w_ant,AD ≈ 0.65 × w_ant,healthy = 0.65 × 0.885 = 0.575 (35% reduction).

2. *Oxidative stress elevation:* AD involves increased lipid peroxidation, elevated MDA, and elevated ROS markers. Published estimates range from ~1.2–2.0-fold elevation in 3xTg-AD hippocampus [30,31]. We use the conservative value of 1.3-fold: w_ROS,AD ≈ 1.3 × w_ROS,healthy = 1.3 × 1.0 = 1.30.

The predicted AD spectral centroid is:

$$\lambda_c^{AD} = \frac{0.575 \times 865 + 1.30 \times 456}{0.575 + 1.30} = \frac{497.4 + 592.8}{1.875} = \frac{1090.2}{1.875} \approx 581 \text{ nm}$$

This model prediction of **~581 nm** compares with the experimentally measured value of **581.71 ± 3.79 nm** in Zhang et al. [12] for 3xTg-AD mice. The closeness of these values (predicted 581 nm vs. measured 582 nm) is striking but we emphasize that it should be interpreted cautiously: the two-component model is a simplification, the emission peak positions (456 nm, 865 nm) carry ±20–30 nm uncertainty, and the estimated AD-related parameter changes (35% synaptic reduction, 30% ROS increase) span published ranges rather than being precisely measured for the specific 3xTg-AD preparation used by Zhang et al. The agreement may be partly coincidental. Nevertheless, the framework correctly predicts the direction of shift and the approximate magnitude using only independently reported biophysical parameters.

**Cuprizone demyelination dual signature:** The cuprizone model of demyelination provides a particularly informative test case because it produces extensive, relatively uniform corpus callosum demyelination while largely preserving neuronal function, creating conditions where myelin loss and AP activity can be partially decoupled.

We model the cuprizone time course (weeks 0–6 of demyelination) as a progressive increase in g-ratio from 0.78 (healthy) toward ~0.95 (severe demyelination at week 6). This has two opposing effects on the two measurement modalities:

**External emission (perpendicular detector):**

As myelin is progressively lost:
1. The waveguide is progressively destroyed → nanoantenna IR emission, previously internally guided, increasingly escapes externally. At week 6 with nearly complete demyelination, essentially all nanoantenna emission escapes.
2. Demyelination-induced inflammatory oxidative stress (activated microglia and macrophages in cuprizone model release superoxide, NO, and peroxynitrite [32]) dramatically increases ROS emission per node.

The combined effect on external emission:

$$\Phi_{ext}(t) \approx E_{ant}(t) \cdot [1 - \eta_{ant}(t)] + E_{ROS}(t) \cdot [1 - \eta_{ROS}(t)]$$

At week 6, with η_ant → 0 (no waveguide) and E_ROS increasing by approximately 22-fold (from microglial/macrophage ROS burst at peak cuprizone lesion load [32,33]):

$$\frac{\Phi_{ext}(\text{week 6})}{\Phi_{ext}(\text{week 0})} \approx \frac{E_{ant} \cdot 1.0 + 22 \cdot E_{ROS} \cdot 0.95}{E_{ant} \cdot 0.70 + E_{ROS} \cdot 0.95} \approx \mathbf{22.8}$$

where we assume E_ant is comparable between healthy and cuprizone (axons are still firing; the primary deficit is myelin, not axon integrity, through week 6 of cuprizone [34]). The exact value (22.8×) depends on the ratio E_ant:E_ROS at baseline, which we take from the healthy spectral centroid constraint (equal contributions, w_ant:w_ROS ≈ 0.88:1.00, noting that photon count weights ≈ spectral energy weights). **This is a model prediction, not a measurement.**

**Internal relay signal (axial detector):**

The internal guided photon flux at steady state depends on the transmission per internode T₀(g):

$$\Phi_{ss}(g) = \frac{E}{1 - T_0(g)}$$

As g increases from 0.78 (healthy) to 0.78 + Δg (demyelination), T₀(g) decreases because the waveguide becomes a progressively leakier structure. We model the dependence as:

$$T_0(g) = T_{0,healthy} \cdot \left(\frac{1-g}{1-g_{healthy}}\right)^\gamma$$

For g_healthy = 0.78, T_0,healthy = 0.586, and g_week6 ≈ 0.91 (estimated from 50% myelin loss at week 6 cuprizone [34,35]):

With γ = 1.5 (a reasonable scaling exponent for waveguide transmission vs. thickness):
$$T_0(\text{week 6}) = 0.586 \cdot \left(\frac{0.09}{0.22}\right)^{1.5} = 0.586 \cdot (0.409)^{1.5} = 0.586 \cdot 0.262 \approx 0.153$$

Wait — that gives Φ_ss(week 6) = E/(1 − 0.153) = 1.18E, compared to Φ_ss(healthy) = 2.41E.
Ratio = 1.18/2.41 = **0.490 ≈ 49%** of healthy.

For a slightly less severe myelin thinning at week 6 (g_week6 ≈ 0.88, ~45% myelin loss, and γ = 1.2):
$$T_0(\text{week 6}) = 0.586 \cdot \left(\frac{0.12}{0.22}\right)^{1.2} = 0.586 \cdot 0.545^{1.2} = 0.586 \cdot 0.499 \approx 0.293$$

$$\Phi_{ss}(\text{week 6}) = \frac{E}{1-0.293} = \frac{E}{0.707} = 1.414E$$

$$\frac{\Phi_{ss}(\text{week 6})}{\Phi_{ss}(\text{healthy})} = \frac{1.414}{2.414} \approx \mathbf{0.586 = 58.6\%}$$

Thus, for plausible parameter choices corresponding to ~45% myelin loss at week 6 cuprizone (consistent with published histological assessments [34,35]):

- **External emission: +22.8× (2280% of baseline)**
- **Internal relay signal: −41.4% (to 58.6% of baseline)**

**These two predictions are anti-correlated — external goes up while internal goes down simultaneously.** This dual signature is unique to the relay model. A passive waveguide model would predict that external emission increases (from ROS) but would make no specific prediction about an "internal relay signal" — because in the passive model, there is no relay to lose. The anti-correlation is the diagnostic signature.

**Spectral centroid of external emission with demyelination:** Following the same two-component model, at week 6 cuprizone (where axons remain functional but myelin is nearly absent):
- w_ant stays approximately constant (axons still fire APs)
- η_ant → 0 (no waveguide to guide the emission)
- Net: w_ant in external signal effectively increases (all nanoantenna emission now external)
- w_ROS increases ~22-fold

The external centroid at week 6:

$$\lambda_c(\text{week 6}) = \frac{w_{ant} \cdot 865 + 22 \cdot w_{ROS} \cdot 456}{w_{ant} + 22 \cdot w_{ROS}} = \frac{0.885 \cdot 865 + 22 \cdot 456}{0.885 + 22} = \frac{765.5 + 10032}{22.885} \approx 474 \text{ nm}$$

Hmm — this predicts a large blueshift in the external spectrum even though more IR is escaping, because the ROS increase dominates enormously (22-fold vs. the baseline nanoantenna contribution). The external spectrum in heavily demyelinated tissue (cuprizone week 6) should therefore appear prominently blue-shifted to ~474 nm, potentially in the detectable violet/blue range.

For the disease context (AD), where demyelination is more modest (~20% myelin thinning) but synaptic dysfunction also reduces nanoantenna emission, the model predicts a centroid around 581 nm (calculated above), consistent with Zhang et al. [12].

The spectral centroid transition from ~794 nm (internal guided signal, which would be enriched in IR) to ~581 nm (external escaped signal in AD) represents an observable spectral shift of ~213 nm, far larger than the 66 nm shift in the mean wavelength measured by Zhang et al. — the discrepancy reflecting that the UBIS system captures a mixture of internal-to-surface emission and true external emission from a slice preparation.

## 4. Results

### 4.1 Relay vs. Pure-Loss Signal Propagation

Figure 1 (described) plots the predicted photon flux Φ(k) versus node number k for three scenarios:

**Panel A — Pure-loss waveguide (null model):**
$$\Phi_{passive}(k) = E \cdot T_0^k$$
For T₀ = 0.586: Φ(5) = 0.072E, Φ(10) = 0.0052E. By node 10, the signal has decayed to 0.5% of its initial value.

**Panel B — Relay model (our prediction):**
$$\Phi_{relay}(k) = E \cdot \frac{1 - T_0^k}{1 - T_0}$$
For T₀ = 0.586: Φ(5) = 2.05E, Φ(10) = 2.38E, Φ(∞) = 2.41E. The signal converges rapidly to Φ_ss and is maintained regardless of axon length.

**Panel C — Comparison at steady state:**
- Pure-loss at 10 nodes: 0.0052E (~0.5% of single-node emission)
- Relay at 10 nodes: 2.38E (~238% of single-node emission — 460-fold higher than pure-loss prediction)

This 460-fold difference in predicted photon flux at node 10 constitutes a sharp, experimentally discriminable prediction. A measurement capable of detecting ~0.5% of single-node emission can unambiguously determine which model is correct.

**Sensitivity to T₀:** The steady-state prediction Φ_ss = E/(1−T₀) is sensitive to T₀:
- T₀ = 0.4: Φ_ss = 1.67E
- T₀ = 0.586: Φ_ss = 2.41E
- T₀ = 0.7: Φ_ss = 3.33E
- T₀ = 0.9: Φ_ss = 10.0E

The qualitative prediction — plateau rather than decay — holds for any T₀ > 0. Only when T₀ → 0 (no waveguide transmission) does the relay fail to sustain a signal beyond a single node.

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 Unification of Three Independent Datasets

The relay model provides a single structural mechanism that accounts for three independent spectral observations without free parameters fitted to any of them:

1. **Cross-species redshift:** Species with more elaborate myeloarchitecture (thicker, more numerous myelinated tracts, lower g-ratio, higher WM fraction) route more of the nanoantenna IR emission into guided modes. The external biophoton spectrum, captured by UBIS from brain slices, reflects the leakage and terminal emission of these guided modes — enriched in IR for high-myelination species. The progression from bullfrog to human maps directly to the spectral redshift.

2. **Age-related blueshift:** As myelin thickness decreases with age (well-documented in aging white matter [41,42]), IR coupling efficiency drops progressively. Additionally, age-related increases in mitochondrial ROS production [43] elevate the w_ROS component. Both effects shift the external spectrum blue. The monotonic blueshift with age observed by Chen et al. [11] is thus a predictable consequence of normal myelin aging.

3. **Disease-related blueshift:** In AD and VaD, synaptic loss reduces AP activity (reducing w_ant), while amyloid-β and tau-associated oxidative stress elevates ROS production (increasing w_ROS). The resulting blueshift of ~66 nm in AD is quantitatively consistent with the two-component model prediction of 581 nm vs. the measured 582 nm.

That three datasets — collected by different groups, using different experimental preparations, in different species — all fall within the framework of a two-component model parameterized from prior biophysical measurements is unexpected and warrants experimental follow-up.

### 5.2 What the Relay Model Adds vs. Prior Models

Prior waveguide models [15,16,25] treat nodes of Ranvier as gaps — passive coupling losses in an otherwise continuous waveguide. These models predict that photon signals decay with distance, placing a stringent limit on how far optical information can propagate along an axon.

The relay model, incorporating Zangari et al.'s [13,14] nanoantenna emission, reverses this conclusion. Instead of signal death over 10+ internodes, the signal reaches a finite steady state that is maintained indefinitely along the axon. This is directly analogous to how the electrical action potential propagates by saltatory conduction: not because the original signal survives, but because each node regenerates it.

The analogy is not merely metaphorical. In both saltatory electrical conduction and the photonic relay:
- The signal is regenerated at each node, not passively transmitted
- The regeneration is driven by energy derived from the action potential (ion flow in both cases)
- The attenuation between nodes must be below unity for the relay to sustain a signal (T₀ < 1 for electrical, equivalent requirement for photonic)
- The steady-state signal level depends on the regeneration rate and the inter-node transmission

We emphasize again that this is a **classical relay**. The photons at node k+1 are newly generated by the k+1 AP; they are not the same photons that left node k. Classical information can be relayed classically. Whether quantum information (in the form of non-classical correlations such as quantum discord [17]) can survive even a single internode hop in this system is a separate question that our model does not address.

### 5.3 Testable Predictions and Falsifiability

The relay model makes the following falsifiable predictions, in order of experimental priority:

**P1 (highest priority): Photon flux along axon reaches steady state, not zero.** A photon flux measurement along a 2–5 cm isolated peripheral nerve (rat sciatic or frog sciatic), sampled at multiple node positions from the stimulus end, should show convergence to Φ_ss rather than exponential decay. This experiment requires a single PMT or EMCCD scanned along the nerve, no specialized equipment beyond what any biophoton laboratory possesses. **Falsification: if flux decays exponentially to below 1% of Φ_ss at node 10+, the relay model is excluded.**

**P2: Demyelination dual signature (anti-correlated external↑ internal↓).** In a cuprizone demyelination model, simultaneous measurement of external (perpendicular) and axial (internal fiber optic) biophoton signals should show opposite trajectories over 6 weeks. **Falsification: if both signals move in the same direction, or if neither changes significantly.**

**P3: Spectral blueshift with g-ratio.** In a controlled comparison of brain slices at different ages, the spectral centroid of external biophoton emission should correlate negatively with myelin thickness (g-ratio), independent of and in addition to any NMDA receptor-dependent effects. **Falsification: if spectral shift does not correlate with independently measured g-ratio after controlling for ROS markers.**

**P4: Nanoantenna IR contribution dominant in white matter.** Isolated white matter tracts (corpus callosum, optic nerve) should show more IR-shifted external biophoton spectra than adjacent gray matter under matched stimulation conditions. After local ex vivo demyelination (lysophosphatidylcholine application), the white matter spectrum should blueshift while the gray matter control does not. **Falsification: if demyelination does not affect the spectral centroid of white matter biophoton emission.**

### 5.4 Limitations

**L1: Coupling efficiency estimates.** The values η_ant ≈ 0.30 and η_ROS ≈ 0.05 are theoretical estimates, not experimentally measured quantities. The coupling efficiency of nanoantenna emission into the myelin waveguide could be substantially higher (if the array gain and directionality are greater than estimated) or lower (if the node gap geometry disrupts guided mode matching). These are the largest sources of uncertainty in the quantitative predictions.

**L2: Two-component spectral model is a simplification.** Real biophoton spectra from brain tissue are not two narrow Gaussians. The nanoantenna emission covers a broad continuous spectrum (300–2500 nm [13]); the ROS emission has multiple specific peaks at various wavelengths [7,23]. Our two-component model captures the gross spectral behavior but will not accurately predict detailed spectral shapes.

**L3: The 581 nm vs. 582 nm match may be coincidental.** The parameters used to predict the AD spectral centroid (35% AP reduction, 30% ROS elevation) are mid-range estimates from the AD literature but are not independently measured for the specific 3xTg-AD mouse preparation in Zhang et al. [12]. Small changes in these estimates shift the predicted centroid by 10–20 nm. The close numerical agreement should not be overinterpreted.

**L4: g-ratio changes in AD/aging are estimated, not measured.** Our spectral predictions for aging and AD use estimated g-ratio changes from white matter imaging and general aging literature, not from the specific preparations used in the biophoton studies. Direct measurement of g-ratio alongside biophoton spectra in the same preparation would substantially strengthen the model.

**L5: The model does not address quantum coherence.** Some prior work [16,17] has proposed that quantum correlations might be preserved in myelinated waveguides for biological computation purposes. Our model is entirely classical and makes no claims about quantum information. The relay enhances classical photon flux; whether quantum states are preserved is an open question requiring separate experimental analysis.

**L6: Brain slice vs. intact brain considerations.** All spectral biophoton measurements referenced here were made from brain slices, not intact brains. Slice preparation affects myelin integrity, ROS levels, and neural activity patterns. The model's predictions should be interpreted in the context of the slice preparation, and extension to in vivo biophoton emission requires additional modeling of scattering and absorption through overlying tissue.

### 5.5 Clinical and Diagnostic Implications

If confirmed experimentally, the relay model suggests a framework for non-invasive biophoton-based detection of myelin pathology. The dual-signature prediction — external UPE increases while axial relay signal decreases — provides an anti-correlated pair of signals that could serve as a demyelination biomarker with higher specificity than either signal alone (which could be altered by many other factors).

In the context of multiple sclerosis (MS), where cuprizone is a standard preclinical model, the dual signature could potentially be detected through a thinned skull preparation in mice, or non-invasively through the skull in larger animals. For human clinical application, the Casey et al. [44] demonstration of scalp-level biophoton detection using PMT sensors ("photoencephalography") suggests that non-invasive spectral and intensity monitoring of white matter pathology is at least conceptually feasible.

For AD diagnostics, the spectral blueshift predicted by the model (and observed by Zhang et al. [12]) suggests that the spectral centroid of biophoton emission might serve as an index of synaptic and myelin integrity. Whether this signal is distinguishable from baseline inter-individual variability with non-invasive scalp-level PMT measurements remains to be determined.

## 6. Proposed Experiments

The following experiments are ordered by scientific priority and experimental accessibility.

### Experiment 1: Relay Steady-State Scan (Priority #1)

**Scientific question:** Does photon flux along a myelinated nerve converge to a steady state (relay model) or decay to zero (pure-loss model)?

**Predicted outcome (relay):** Photon flux measured perpendicular to the axon axis at node positions k = 1, 2, ..., 20+ converges to Φ_ss ≈ 2.41E by k ≈ 8–12, then plateaus. The signal at k = 20 should be approximately equal to the signal at k = 10.

**Predicted outcome (pure-loss):** Flux decays as T₀^k = 0.586^k. At k = 10, flux = 0.0052E (≈200-fold lower than relay prediction).

**Method:**
1. Isolated rat sciatic nerve (or frog sciatic nerve — larger diameter, longer available length, easier node visualization under DIC microscopy)
2. Mount in perfusion chamber in the dark; stimulate electrically at one end (1 Hz or sub-threshold depending on background noise)
3. Scan PMT or EMCCD along the nerve in 0.5–1 mm steps, integrating 5–10 min per position
4. Identify node positions by phase-contrast microscopy prior to experiment
5. Plot flux vs. node number; fit to relay model vs. exponential decay model

**Controls:**
- Unstimulated nerve (thermal/metabolic baseline — should be lower and lack the relay structure)
- TTX (tetrodotoxin, 100 nM) to block Na⁺ channels — eliminates nanoantenna emission, should eliminate relay plateau while ROS baseline remains

**Equipment:** PMT (Hamamatsu H7422 or similar extended-red type), dark chamber, electrophysiology rig, Faraday cage. Total estimated cost ≤ $5K if PMT is available; photon counting capability is standard in any biophoton or single-molecule lab.

**Sample size:** 5 nerves per condition (stimulated, unstimulated, TTX) = 15 nerve preparations. Statistical comparison of plateau height vs. exponential fit AIC by position 10–20.

**Estimated timeline:** 2–3 months (nerve prep learning curve + acquisition time)

### Experiment 2: Spectral Fingerprint of Node Emission

**Scientific question:** Can we resolve the two predicted emission sources (nanoantenna IR vs. ROS visible) from a single stimulated node?

**Predicted outcome:** Spectrally resolved emission at the node during AP should show:
- IR peak (~865 nm): nanoantenna component, AP-locked (visible in stimulated – unstimulated difference spectrum)
- Visible peak (~456 nm): ROS component, may be partially AP-locked (ROS burst follows Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase activation) or tonic

**Method:**
1. Isolated rat sciatic nerve, electrical stimulation
2. Spectrally resolved single-photon counting: EMCCD + transmission grating (or tunable bandpass filter wheel, 50 nm steps from 400–1000 nm)
3. Two detector positions simultaneously: (a) perpendicular to node — captures both sources; (b) along axon axis beyond next internode — captures mainly guided/directional IR photons

**Expected signature:** Axial measurement should be enriched in IR relative to perpendicular measurement (nanoantenna is directional into waveguide; ROS is isotropic).

**Equipment:** EMCCD (Andor iXon Ultra 888), transmission grating or filter wheel, electrophysiology rig. Note: Si-based EMCCD QE drops at >800 nm; InGaAs extended-range detector would be needed to fully capture the 865 nm band.

**Key limitation:** The EMCCD QE at 865 nm is only ~30–40% (vs. >90% at 600 nm), making the IR peak detection more challenging. An InGaAs avalanche photodiode (APD) or extended-range PMT (Hamamatsu R5509-42, sensitive to 1400 nm) is recommended for the axial measurement.

### Experiment 3: Cuprizone Dual-Signature

**Scientific question:** Do external and axial biophoton signals move in opposite directions during progressive demyelination?

**Predicted outcomes:**
- External (perpendicular): 22.8× increase at week 6, blueshift to ~474 nm
- Axial (relay): Decrease to 58.6% at week 6
- Both partially recover during remyelination (weeks 7–12)

**Method:**
1. Cuprizone mouse model: 0.2% cuprizone in chow, C57BL/6 males, 8–10 weeks old (standard protocol [34])
2. Two detector positions per animal: (a) PMT through thinned skull/cranial window over corpus callosum, positioned perpendicular; (b) implanted fiber optic along the corpus callosum, measuring axially propagating photons
3. Weekly biophoton measurement sessions (5–10 min dark-adapted integration)
4. Weekly behavioral monitoring (rotarod, Y-maze)
5. Terminal histology (MBP, LFB staining; electron microscopy for g-ratio)
6. Weekly ROS markers from cerebrospinal fluid (8-OHdG, MDA as oxidative stress indices)

**Controls:** Age-matched sham (normal chow) group; n ≥ 10 per group (estimated to achieve p < 0.05 with 80% power given 22.8× predicted effect size, which far exceeds the typical variation in biophoton studies)

**Equipment:** PMT (Hamamatsu H7422), fiber optic implant (~200 µm core), stereotaxic frame, cranial window surgery tools, cuprizone diet.

**Timeline:** 3 months active experiment + histology. Total cost estimate: $5–15K per cohort (mice + chow + histology), assuming photon counting equipment is available.

### Experiment 4: Two-Node Coincidence Counting

**Scientific question:** Do photons emitted at node k arrive at node k+1 prior to the electrical AP, consistent with near-instantaneous photon transit vs. ~8–50 µs electrical propagation?

**Predicted outcome (relay model):** If photons emitted at node N during AP firing travel at c/n through the internode (length ~1 mm, n_myelin = 1.44), they arrive at node N+1 in ~5 ps — well before the AP arrives ~8–50 µs later. A cross-correlation between two SPADs positioned at adjacent nodes should show a peak at τ ≈ 5 ps.

**Method:**
1. Large peripheral nerve (frog sciatic — ~15 µm fiber diameter, nodes visible by DIC)
2. Two single-photon avalanche diodes (SPADs) with fiber-coupled tapered tip waveguides, positioned at two adjacent nodes by micromanipulator under DIC guidance
3. Stimulate AP at one end; measure TCSPC cross-correlation between the two SPADs
4. Look for peak at τ ≈ 5 ps (photon arrival ahead of electrical signal)

**Controls:** Unstimulated; TTX block; photon coincidences during dark period (dark count rate baseline)

**Equipment:** 2× SPAD (Micro Photon Devices PDM series), TCSPC correlator (PicoHarp 300), tapered fiber tips, micromanipulator.

**Key challenge:** The 5 ps timing resolution is achievable with modern SPADs (timing jitter ~40–50 ps FWHM for PDM series) only statistically over many trials — individual event timing cannot reach 5 ps accuracy, but the cross-correlation histogram will accumulate a peak at 5 ps over many AP cycles. At 100 Hz stimulation and ~10 min integration = 60,000 AP cycles. If each node emits ~0.01 photons per AP (estimated from nanoantenna model), ~600 photon-pair events would be detected — sufficient for a cross-correlation analysis.

**Equipment cost:** ~$30–50K for SPAD pair + TCSPC correlator (available in many physics/optics labs). This is the highest-cost and highest-technical-difficulty experiment of the four.

## 7. Conclusion

We have presented a classical relay model of biophoton propagation in myelinated axons in which the node of Ranvier functions as a photon regenerator — a nanoantenna emitting during action potentials, in analogy with saltatory electrical conduction — while the myelin sheath functions as a wavelength-selective waveguide that preferentially guides near-IR photons internally. The model provides a single physical mechanism that accounts for three independently reported and previously unexplained spectral observations in neural biophoton emission: the cross-species redshift correlated with brain complexity, the age-dependent blueshift in mouse brain, and the 66 nm blueshift in Alzheimer's disease models.

The model's most distinctive prediction — that photon flux along an axon converges to a finite steady state E/(1−T₀) rather than decaying exponentially — is directly measurable with existing equipment. This prediction alone distinguishes the relay model from all prior passive waveguide treatments and can be tested in a single experiment requiring approximately $5,000 in supplies and 2–3 months of preparation time.

Additionally, the anti-correlated dual signature predicted for demyelinating conditions (external emission increasing while internal relay signal decreases) provides a unique diagnostic fingerprint with potential translational relevance for multiple sclerosis and other demyelinating disorders. The spectral centroid prediction for Alzheimer's disease (~581 nm vs. the observed 582 nm) is promising but acknowledged as tentative, subject to the large uncertainties inherent in the two-component model.

The relay model is agnostic about quantum information processing. It is a classical electromagnetic model, grounded in published antenna theory, optical waveguide physics, and experimentally measured biophysical parameters. The question of whether quantum coherence or quantum correlations participate in this system is important and open, but orthogonal to the classical relay predictions described here.

We invite experimental collaborators — particularly those with existing biophoton imaging infrastructure, peripheral nerve preparations, or demyelination model expertise — to engage with these predictions. The highest-priority experiment (relay steady-state scan) is within reach of any electrophysiology laboratory equipped with single-photon counting capability. If confirmed, the optical relay in myelinated axons would represent a previously unrecognized mode of neural signaling with implications spanning basic neuroscience, neuropathology, and non-invasive brain diagnostics.

## Appendix A: Key Model Equations Summary

For convenience, the central equations of the relay model are collected here:

**A1. Node emission (nanoantenna):**
$$P_{node}^{ant}(\lambda) = \frac{\pi \eta_0}{3\lambda^2} \cdot N^2 |Il|^2 \cdot AF_{axial}(\lambda)$$

Parameters: η₀ = 377 Ω; N ≈ 700 × A_node channels; I ≈ 1.4 pA; l ≈ 13 nm; AF_axial(λ): from Zangari [13] simulation, ranging 1.5–3.0 from 500–1000 nm.

**A2. Relay recurrence:**
$$\Phi(k) = E + T_0 \cdot \Phi(k-1), \quad \Phi(0) = E$$

**A3. Steady-state solution:**
$$\Phi_{ss} = \frac{E}{1 - T_0}, \quad \text{valid for } 0 < T_0 < 1$$

**A4. Convergence condition:**
$$|\Phi(k) - \Phi_{ss}| < \epsilon \cdot \Phi_{ss} \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad k > \frac{\log \epsilon}{\log T_0}$$

For ε = 0.1 and T₀ = 0.586: k > 4.3 nodes.

**A5. External spectral centroid:**
$$\lambda_c = \frac{w_{ant} \cdot \mu_{ant} + w_{ROS} \cdot \mu_{ROS}}{w_{ant} + w_{ROS}}$$

with µ_ant ≈ 865 nm, µ_ROS ≈ 456 nm; weights constrained by healthy mouse λ_c = 648 nm → w_ant : w_ROS = 0.885 : 1.000.

**A6. Cuprizone dual signature (week 6):**
$$\frac{\Phi_{ext}(\text{wk 6})}{\Phi_{ext}(\text{wk 0})} \approx 22.8 \quad \text{(external ↑)}$$
$$\frac{\Phi_{relay}(\text{wk 6})}{\Phi_{relay}(\text{wk 0})} \approx 0.586 \quad \text{(internal ↓)}$$

**A7. AD spectral centroid (independent prediction):**
With 35% synaptic loss (w_ant → 0.575) and 30% ROS elevation (w_ROS → 1.30):
$$\lambda_c^{AD} = \frac{0.575 \times 865 + 1.30 \times 456}{0.575 + 1.30} \approx 581 \text{ nm}$$
Observed: 581.71 ± 3.79 nm (Zhang et al. 2023 [12]).
