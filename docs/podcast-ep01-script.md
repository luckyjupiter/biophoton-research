# Episode 1: "When the Light Goes Out"
## The Biophoton Research Podcast
### Host: Yesh (Joshua Lengfelder)
### Target length: ~22 minutes (~3800 words)

---

[INTRO — 2 min]

What if I told you that your nerves carry light?

Not metaphorically. Not "light" as in enlightenment or consciousness or any of that. I mean actual photons — particles of electromagnetic radiation — traveling through the insulation around your nerve fibers at two hundred million meters per second.

And what if I told you that when you lose that insulation — in diseases like multiple sclerosis, or Guillain-Barré syndrome, which I've had — it's not just your electrical signals that break down? It might be an entire optical communication network going dark.

Welcome to the Biophoton Research Podcast. I'm Yesh. And this first episode is about what happens when the light inside your nerves goes out.

Here's what we're going to cover today. First, the physics — how the myelin sheath around your nerves works like a fiber optic cable, and why this isn't just a loose analogy. Second, what the models predict should happen to that light when myelin is damaged or destroyed. Third, my own experience with Guillain-Barré syndrome, and what it means through this lens. And fourth, the wild part — why nobody has actually tested any of this yet, and what it would take to do so.

I want to be upfront about something from the start. Some of what I'm going to tell you today is well-established physics. Some of it is computational modeling that's been published in serious journals. Some of it is theoretical prediction. And some of it is genuinely speculative. I'll tell you which is which as we go. This isn't a hype show. It's a research show. Let's get into it.

---

[CORE SCIENCE 1 — Myelin as Optical Waveguide — 5 min]

Okay, so — myelin. Most people who've taken a biology class know that myelin is the insulation around nerve fibers. It speeds up electrical signals. Lose it, and nerve conduction slows down or stops. That's the textbook version, and it's correct as far as it goes.

But here's what the textbook doesn't tell you. Myelin has optical properties that are remarkably similar to fiber optic cable. And I don't mean "kind of similar if you squint." I mean the numbers line up.

A fiber optic cable works by trapping light inside a core that has a higher refractive index than its surroundings. Light enters the core, hits the boundary, and bounces back in — total internal reflection. As long as the index contrast is right, the light stays trapped and propagates down the fiber.

Now look at a myelinated nerve fiber. The myelin sheath has a refractive index of about one point four four. The axon inside it — the actual nerve fiber — is about one point three eight. And the fluid surrounding everything is about one point three four. High index layer wrapped around a lower index core, sitting in even lower index surroundings. That's a waveguide.

And multiple independent research groups have confirmed this computationally. Kumar and colleagues at the University of Calgary published the foundational study in 2016 in Scientific Reports, using industry-standard electromagnetic simulation software. They showed that guided modes — specific patterns of light that can propagate stably — exist in the myelin sheath across exactly the wavelength range where biological tissue is known to emit photons. Visible light. Near-infrared. The wavelengths match.

Then Babini and Bhatt at the University of Toledo showed in 2022 that the geometry actually works better as something called an ARROW waveguide — anti-resonant reflecting optical waveguide. Without getting too deep into the physics, this means the myelin layers act like tiny mirrors, reflecting certain wavelengths of light back into the structure while letting others leak out. It's wavelength-selective. Different thicknesses of myelin guide different colors of light.

And here's where it gets really specific. Zeng and colleagues published in Applied Optics in 2022, and they found that each individual layer of myelin shifts the operating wavelength of the waveguide by exactly fifty-two point three nanometers. Each layer. That means a nerve with twenty layers of myelin guides a different wavelength than one with fifteen layers. Your nervous system might be doing what the telecommunications industry calls wavelength-division multiplexing — carrying multiple independent signals on different colors of light through different-caliber nerve fibers.

One more number, because this one blew my mind when I first saw it. Light traveling through myelin moves at roughly two hundred million meters per second. An electrical action potential in a fast myelinated nerve? About a hundred meters per second. That's a factor of two million. Two million times faster. If photons carry any information at all, they arrive effectively instantly on any timescale the brain cares about.

Now — does this actually happen in living nervous systems? That's the big question. And the honest answer is: we have strong computational evidence, some experimental evidence, and a lot of theoretical prediction. Zangari and colleagues detected photons at nodes of Ranvier — the gaps in the myelin sheath — during electrically stimulated nerve activity in 2021. Tang and Bhatt captured photons from active nerves using photographic techniques. The Dai lab in Wuhan has shown that brain tissue emits photons that respond to neurotransmitter stimulation and change with age and disease.

But a complete demonstration — showing photons generated at one end of a myelinated fiber arriving at the other end with information intact — that hasn't been done. Yet.

So we have this remarkable structure that appears to be a biological optical waveguide. The physics works on paper. The simulations confirm it. Photons have been detected in the right places. But the full proof-of-concept experiment remains to be done.

Which brings us to the question that got me into this research in the first place: what happens when the waveguide breaks?

---

[CORE SCIENCE 2 — Demyelination and the Light — 5 min]

When myelin is damaged — thinned, fragmented, or stripped off entirely — the waveguide model makes very specific, quantitative predictions about what should happen to photon transmission. Let me walk through them, because this is where the science gets genuinely exciting.

Prediction one: spectral shift. Remember that fifty-two point three nanometer rule — each layer of myelin shifts the operating wavelength. Run that in reverse. If a disease process removes five layers of myelin, the operating wavelength should blueshift by about two hundred and sixty nanometers. That's the difference between red light and blue light. It's huge. And it's detectable with a basic spectrometer.

Interestingly, this has indirect support already. Chen and colleagues in the Dai lab showed in 2020 that aging mouse brains — which are known to undergo progressive myelin thinning — exhibit a spectral blueshift in their biophoton emission. And Wang and colleagues in 2023 found the same pattern in Alzheimer's disease and vascular dementia models. Both conditions involve white matter degeneration. The spectral shift was there, consistent with the waveguide prediction. Nobody designed those experiments to test the waveguide hypothesis. They just happened to observe what it predicts.

Prediction two: scattering. When myelin fragments — breaks into discontinuous segments, as happens in early Wallerian degeneration or at the edges of MS plaques — each break point acts like cutting a fiber optic cable. You get massive scattering. Standard waveguide theory predicts three to ten decibels of loss per break. In plain English, two or three breaks and you've lost ninety to ninety-nine point nine percent of your signal. The photonic channel is functionally destroyed.

But here's the thing — those scattered photons don't disappear. They leak out sideways, through the surface of the nerve. So a nerve undergoing active fragmentation should actually appear brighter from the outside — more photons escaping laterally — while transmitting fewer photons end-to-end. You'd see bright spots at every break point with dark regions in between.

Prediction three: the oxidative burst. Active demyelination — the kind you see in an MS relapse or in Guillain-Barré — involves a massive inflammatory assault. Macrophages and microglia generate reactive oxygen species that attack the lipid-rich myelin membranes. And lipid peroxidation is one of the primary sources of biophotons in biological tissue.

The photochemistry here is well-characterized. When reactive oxygen species attack lipid membranes, you get specific light-emitting molecular species: triplet excited carbonyls that emit in the three-fifty to five-fifty nanometer range — that's UV to blue-green. And singlet oxygen, which has very specific emission lines at six thirty-four nanometers and seven-oh-three nanometers.

So active demyelination should produce a transient but dramatic increase in photon emission — possibly ten to a hundred times above baseline — with a characteristic spectral fingerprint. Different types of demyelination should even produce different spectra. Autoimmune attack, with its myeloperoxidase-driven chemistry, should look different from toxic demyelination like cuprizone poisoning, which works through mitochondrial stress. The spectral signature could potentially tell you not just that demyelination is happening, but what's causing it.

And then there's the most speculative prediction of all. Liu and colleagues published a paper in Physical Review E in 2024 showing that the cylindrical cavity formed by the myelin sheath can theoretically generate entangled photon pairs — pairs of quantum-correlated photons — from vibrations of carbon-hydrogen bonds in the myelin lipids. Entanglement peaks at biologically realistic myelin thicknesses and drops off as myelin thins. If this is right — and I want to emphasize the "if" — then demyelination doesn't just degrade an optical channel. It destroys a quantum channel. That paper is in a serious physics journal, it's mathematically rigorous, but it's entirely theoretical. Nobody has detected entangled photon pairs from myelin. But the prediction is there, waiting to be tested.

Now here's the part that keeps me up at night. None of these predictions have been tested. Not one. No study has ever measured biophoton emission specifically in a demyelinating disease model. Not in EAE mice — the standard animal model for MS. Not in cuprizone-treated mice, where you can cause and reverse demyelination on a predictable schedule. Not in tissue preparations from patients. The experimental designs are straightforward. The detection technology exists. Standard animal models are available in neuroimmunology labs worldwide. What's been missing is the connection between two fields — photonics and neuroimmunology — that don't normally talk to each other.

[THE PERSONAL STORY — 4 min]

I know what demyelination feels like from the inside. Not in the abstract, published-paper sense. In the "lying in a hospital bed watching my body shut down" sense.

In my case, it was Guillain-Barré syndrome — GBS. An acute autoimmune attack on the peripheral nervous system. The demyelinating form, called AIDP, accounts for about eighty-five to ninety percent of cases. What happens is your immune system — specifically, macrophages — physically strip the myelin off your nerve fibers. They penetrate the Schwann cell basement membrane and peel the myelin away, layer by layer.

The experience is ascending paralysis. It starts in your feet and climbs. You feel your extremities go numb, then weak, then completely unresponsive. Your reflexes vanish. If it climbs high enough, you can't breathe on your own. It is, without exaggeration, one of the most frightening things a nervous system can do to itself.

And then — if you're fortunate, as I was — it reverses. Schwann cells in the peripheral nervous system are remarkably good at remyelination. Over weeks and months, new myelin wraps form around the stripped axons. Sensation returns. Strength returns. You get your body back, piece by piece.

But here's what I didn't know then, and what I can't stop thinking about now: the new myelin is different. This is well-established neuroscience — remyelinated nerve fibers have thinner sheaths and shorter internodes than the originals. Electrophysiologically, this is why some GBS survivors have permanently reduced nerve conduction velocities even after clinical recovery.

But in the waveguide framework? Thinner myelin means a different operating wavelength. Fifty-two point three nanometers per layer, remember? If I lost ten layers and got back seven, my rebuilt peripheral nerves are now operating at a wavelength shifted by about one hundred and fifty-seven nanometers compared to the original. I'm not just electrically repaired. If the waveguide hypothesis is correct, I'm optically retuned. My nervous system is running on a different color of light than it was before.

I want to be clear — I don't know if this matters functionally. Nobody does. Because nobody has measured it. But the prediction is there, written in the physics of dielectric waveguides, and it applies to every single person who has ever recovered from a demyelinating episode.

And the numbers are staggering. Two point eight million people worldwide living with MS. GBS affects one to two people per hundred thousand per year. Add in Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease, leukodystrophies, all the other conditions where myelin is the target — we're talking about millions of people whose nervous systems may have undergone an optical transformation that we've never once measured.

If biophoton spectroscopy could track demyelination and remyelination in real time — and the physics says it should be possible — it would transform how we monitor and treat these diseases. Right now, the best tool we have for assessing myelin is MRI, which requires expensive equipment, takes time, and gives you a snapshot, not continuous monitoring. Imagine instead a photon detector — maybe fiber-coupled, maybe even wearable — that could tell you in real time whether myelin is being lost or rebuilt, and at what rate. That's what the waveguide model points toward.

---

[THE BIGGER PICTURE — 4 min]

Let me zoom out for a minute and put this in context. Because the demyelination angle is just one piece of a much larger question.

The question is: does the brain use light?

Not "does the brain emit light" — we know it does. Ultra-weak photon emission from neural tissue has been measured by dozens of labs going back decades. Brains glow, very faintly, as a byproduct of oxidative metabolism. That's established.

The question is whether that light does anything. Whether it carries information. Whether the nervous system has evolved to use it.

And the evidence is accumulating. In February 2025, a paper in iScience proposed something called "photoencephalography" — using ultraweak photon emissions as a readout of brain activity, the way EEG uses electrical fields. They showed that brain photon emissions change with cognitive tasks, differ from background in their spectral and entropy properties, and correlate with neural oscillations. That's not proof that photons carry information between neurons, but it shows they're not just random metabolic noise.

The Zarkeshian model from 2022 — published in Scientific Reports — went further. It proposed that biophotons propagating through myelinated axons could enable backpropagation-like learning in neural circuits. Backpropagation is the algorithm that makes deep learning work in artificial intelligence. If biological brains use something similar, and if photons are the medium, then demyelination doesn't just slow your nerve conduction. It degrades your brain's ability to learn.

Now, I need to pump the brakes here because this is deep speculation territory. The backpropagation hypothesis hasn't been tested. We don't have direct evidence that biophotons enable learning. What we have is a mathematical model showing it's physically plausible.

But there's a pattern in neuroscience where things we thought were simple turn out to be complex, and structures we thought had one function turn out to have several. Glial cells were "just" support cells until we discovered they actively modulate neural signaling. Dendrites were passive cables until we discovered dendritic computation. Myelin was "just" insulation. Maybe it's also a waveguide. Maybe it's also a quantum optical cavity. We won't know until we look.

And that's exactly what this research program is about. Taking the theoretical predictions seriously, designing rigorous experiments, and actually measuring what happens to photon emission when myelin is damaged and repaired.

The experiments are not science fiction. EAE mice — the standard MS model — develop optic neuritis, inflammation of the optic nerve. The optic nerve is one of the most heavily myelinated structures in the central nervous system, and it's surgically accessible. You dissect it, mount it in a perfusion chamber, stimulate it electrically, and measure photon emission with a cooled EM-CCD camera. Compare healthy versus demyelinated nerves. Look for the spectral shift, the scattering pattern, the oxidative burst. The equipment exists. The animal models are standard. What's missing is someone to connect the dots.

Meanwhile, on the therapy side, things are finally moving. The CCMR-Two trial, presented at ECTRIMS in September 2025, showed that combining metformin and clemastine significantly increased myelin repair in relapsing-remitting MS patients. The effects were small, and there were no disability improvements after six months — remyelination takes time to translate into function. But it's the third trial showing this combination works. Remyelination is becoming a real therapeutic target, not just a theoretical one.

And that makes the measurement problem even more urgent. Because how do you know your remyelination therapy is working if you can't measure remyelination in real time? MRI can do it, sort of, with advanced sequences like myelin water fraction imaging. But it's expensive, slow, and indirect. A biophoton-based approach could potentially give you continuous, myelin-specific monitoring at a fraction of the cost.

[OUTRO — 2 min]

Let me bring this back to where we started.

Your nerves almost certainly carry light. The physics is clear, the simulations confirm it, and photons have been detected in the right places. Whether that light carries meaningful information — whether the nervous system has evolved to use it — is still an open question. A fascinating, testable, open question.

What we know for sure: myelin has the optical properties of a waveguide. When it's damaged, the waveguide physics predicts specific, measurable changes in photon emission. Those predictions have never been tested in a demyelination model. Not once.

What we're building toward: a research program that bridges photonics and neuroimmunology to make those measurements. Starting with animal models. Working toward clinical tools. Open science, open data, open collaboration.

If you're a neuroscientist, a photonics researcher, a clinician who works with demyelinating diseases, or someone who — like me — has lived through the experience of having your myelin attacked and rebuilt, this work intersects with yours. And I'd love to hear from you.

You can find this project on Telegram at @biophotonresearch. That's where the research discussions happen, where new findings get posted, and where collaborators connect. Everything we're doing is being developed in the open.

We've spent a century understanding myelin as electrical insulation. Maybe it's time to turn on the light.

Thanks for listening. I'm Yesh, and this has been the Biophoton Research Podcast.
